# 30-60-90 Day Plan for QA Engineers

Use this in final rounds when asked: "What would your first 90 days look like?"
Or bring it up proactively: "I have been thinking about how I would approach the first 90 days — would it be useful to walk you through my thinking?"

Customize this with specifics from what you have learned about the company throughout your interviews.

---

## The Golden Rule of a 90-Day Plan

**First 30 days: Listen and learn. Do not fix anything yet.**

Nothing signals poor judgment faster than someone who joins and immediately starts changing things before they understand the system. The best 90-day plans show that you know how to earn trust before you try to exercise influence.

---

## First 30 Days — Orient and Understand

### Goals
- Build relationships with the team
- Understand the current state of quality and testing
- Learn the product deeply as a user
- Understand the SDLC and release process
- Identify where the biggest quality risks are (but do not announce them yet — keep listening)

### Specific Actions

**Week 1**
- Meet 1:1 with every QA team member — ask about their day-to-day, what is working, what is frustrating
- Meet with at least 2-3 developers — understand their perspective on QA
- Meet with product managers — understand how requirements flow
- Get access to all QA tools (test management, bug tracker, CI/CD dashboard, monitoring)
- Read the existing test plans, test strategies, and any QA documentation

**Week 2**
- Shadow a full sprint from planning to release — observe, do not judge
- Run the existing test suite from scratch on your machine — document what breaks
- Read the last 3 months of bug reports — look for patterns
- Review the CI/CD pipeline and understand what tests run and when
- Identify the highest-risk areas of the product

**Week 3-4**
- Write up your observations — a personal document, not for distribution yet
- Start contributing to actual testing work — take on test cases and get context
- Identify 1-2 quick wins that would be easy to implement with minimal disruption
- Have a sync with your manager: share initial observations and align on priorities

### What Success Looks Like at Day 30
- You know the product, the team, the process, and the codebase well enough to be dangerous
- Your colleagues trust you
- You have formed a clear picture of what needs to change and in what order

---

## Days 31-60 — Contribute and Begin Improving

### Goals
- Become a reliable, productive member of the team
- Implement the quick wins you identified in month 1
- Begin proposing medium-term improvements — with data, not just opinions
- Build enough credibility that your perspective carries weight

### Specific Actions

**Testing Execution**
- Own test cycles for at least 1-2 major features independently
- Write test cases and test plans without supervision
- Contribute meaningfully to sprint testing and bug triage

**Process Improvements**
- Propose 1-2 specific, scoped improvements based on your month-1 observations
- Back every proposal with data: "I reviewed the last 3 months of production bugs and found that X% came from [area]. Here is what I think we should do about it."
- Get buy-in from your manager before announcing anything to the wider team

**Automation**
- If there are automation gaps, write a short proposal for what to automate first and why
- Contribute to the existing automation suite — fix flaky tests, add coverage to gaps
- Understand the full CI/CD integration and where tests run

**Relationships**
- Be the QA voice in sprint planning and grooming
- Start pair testing with developers to build trust and catch issues earlier
- Attend cross-functional meetings to understand the broader product context

### What Success Looks Like at Day 60
- You are executing independently without hand-holding
- You have shipped at least 1 meaningful improvement
- Your manager trusts your judgment
- Your developer partners see you as a quality advocate, not just a bug-finder

---

## Days 61-90 — Drive and Scale

### Goals
- Begin driving quality improvements, not just executing them
- Have a clear view of the 6-month QA roadmap
- Establish yourself as the go-to quality voice on your team(s)
- Identify the 1-2 biggest strategic bets for improving quality at scale

### Specific Actions

**Strategy**
- Draft a QA strategy document for the next 6 months
- Prioritize automation coverage, process improvements, and tooling investments
- Present it to your manager and get feedback — revise and align

**Mentorship and Team**
- If there are junior QA engineers, begin mentoring them
- Document processes that only exist in people's heads
- Build or improve the team's onboarding documentation

**Metrics**
- Propose a set of quality metrics the team will track going forward
- Get baseline measurements in place
- Create a simple dashboard or report for stakeholder visibility

**Cross-Functional Impact**
- Run a quality review with product and engineering — share findings from the first 90 days
- Propose changes to how QA is integrated into the SDLC
- Champion shift-left testing practices if they are not in place

### What Success Looks Like at Day 90
- Your manager sees you as someone who came in, learned fast, earned trust, and is now driving real improvement
- You have a 6-month plan that is aligned with leadership
- The team is in a better quality position than when you arrived
- You are confident, contributing, and known

## How to Present This in an Interview

**Short version (2 minutes):**
"In the first 30 days I would focus entirely on listening and learning — meeting the team, understanding the product, reviewing existing test coverage and bug history. I would resist the urge to fix anything until I really understand the system. In days 30-60 I would start contributing fully and implementing small, high-confidence improvements. By day 90 I would want to have a clear quality strategy for the next 6 months, aligned with engineering and product leadership."

**Customized version:**
"Based on what you have told me about [specific challenge the company mentioned], my first 30 days would be focused especially on [relevant area]. I know you mentioned [specific gap] is a priority, and I would want to understand that deeply before proposing any solutions."

---

## Customization Checklist

Before your final round, fill in the blanks:

- [ ] What specific challenge did the hiring manager mention? → Focus on that area in your Day 30-60 section
- [ ] What tools does the company use? → Reference them by name
- [ ] What is the team size and structure? → Adjust mentorship/collaboration accordingly
- [ ] Is automation a priority for them? → Weight your automation plan accordingly
- [ ] What stage is the company at (early startup vs enterprise)? → Adjust speed and scope of improvements
